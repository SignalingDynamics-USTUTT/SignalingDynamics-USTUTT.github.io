<h4>Background</h4>
<p>Cancer is a leading cause of death. Despite the development of increasingly effective targeted therapies, cancer inevitably strikes back, typically via a few cells acquiring genetic mutations that bypass the oncogenic inhibition. Understanding <b>which</b> and <b>how</b> cells acquire such resistance is necessary for developing therapies that better expand patients' quality and quantity of life.</p>

<p>While recent technical advances enables accurate profiling of single cancer cells at fixed timepoints, little is known about the temporal dynamics inbetween. To overcome this critical limitation, my collaborators and I developed innovative time-lapse microscopy and automated image-analysis algorithms (<a href="/publication">Chen <em>et al. Nat Commun</em> 2021; Tian <em>et al. Cell Rep</em> 2020</a>), allowing us for the first time to continuously monitor thousands of single drug-treated cancer cells in their native states over two weeks. Applying them to BRAF-driven melanoma cells, we identified an unexpected subpopulation ("escapees") that rapidly escaped the action of clinically approved BRAF inhibitors and entered a slow-cycling state via non-genetic mechanisms. While these cells out-proliferated non-escapees, they harbored increased DNA damage and thus were more likely to acquire new genetic mutations. Together, our work demonstrate that escapees could be the seed population driving cancer recurrence.</p>
</p>

<h4>Current Research</h4>
<img src="../images/research/current_research.png" alt="Overview of Current Research" style="max-width:100%;">
<p>
The overarching goal of our research is to systematically characterize single drug-treated cancer cells throughout the entire timeline of recurrence, from the time of drug addition to the formation of resistant colonies. Specifically, we will pursue three research directions.
</p>


<p><b>Expanding the boundaries of long-term time-lapse microscopy across space and time.</b> We will develop experimental and computational techniques that enable month-long monitoring of single cancer cells in both 2D and 3D <em>in-vitro</em> culture models.</p>



I will develop experimental and computational technologies enabling month-long monitoring of single cancer cells in both 2D and 3D in vitro culturing models. Experimentally, I will develop novel culturing models, live-cell reporters, and microscopy techniques to accurately measure the activity of signaling pathways without high toxicity on the cells. Computationally, I will improve the accuracy of my existing cell segmentation and tracking software with state-of-the-art deep-learning algorithms. Emphasis on speed and user-friendliness will facilitate the democratization of the technology. This objective will greatly benefit from the USTUTT’s expertise in engineering disciplines. In return, my technology could be used to study many biological processes at cellular scales, enabling broad collaboration with basic biology researchers. Thus, this objective forms the core capacity for my integration within the USTUTT’s “Biomedical Systems” research profile.</b><br>
</p>
